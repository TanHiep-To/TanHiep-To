Lessons learned from shipping AI projects

What I learned from building RAG systems, recommender pipelines, and ML products.

May 2026 Tags: Projects · Delivery · MLOps Reading time: 4 min

Transitioning from completing academic assignments to deploying real-world AI systems for thousands of users requires a fundamental paradigm shift. Throughout the process of building complex RAG architectures, Recommender Systems, and Cybersecurity threat detection models, I have paid the price for system crashes, model drift, and unacceptable latency. Below are the most invaluable lessons I have learned and carry with me into every new project.

1. Define the Exact Problem Before Writing Code

A classic mistake made by junior AI engineers (myself included in the past) is immediately jumping to implement the latest State-of-the-Art (SOTA) model. The reality is, if you do not deeply understand the business objective (e.g., reducing user churn, increasing click-through rates, or minimizing false positives in security), your model will be useless. Before training anything, I now sit down with the Product Manager to align on: "What exactly defines a good outcome?" and "What is our strict maximum latency budget?"

2. A Stable Data Pipeline Outweighs a Complex Model

A simple model (like Logistic Regression or XGBoost) trained on a clean, automatically updated, and properly versioned data stream will always outperform a complex Deep Learning model trained on a stale, messy CSV file. Reproducibility is the absolute key. If you cannot exactly reproduce the model weights you deployed last Tuesday, your system is facing a severe operational risk.

3. Automated Evaluation is Your Shield

Never deploy a new model solely because it achieved a higher F1-score on a static test set. When working with AI Search, we built a highly scrutinized Offline Evaluation Set (Golden Set). Every new model candidate had to pass through this dataset within the CI/CD pipeline. Furthermore, utilizing Canary Releases (routing only 5% of live traffic to the new model) is essential for catching bizarre real-world edge cases that a static test set can never fully cover.

4. Engineering is the Art of Trade-offs

In AI, you can never have it all. Increasing model complexity inherently increases inference cost and latency. In the Cybersecurity domain, I learned that a model with 95% accuracy running in 10ms is always prioritized over a 98% accurate model that takes 500ms to execute. Always document your Architecture Decision Records (ADRs) to explain exactly why the team chose to sacrifice 1% accuracy in exchange for a 400ms reduction in latency.

Ultimately, bringing AI to production is not just about math and neural network architectures. It is 80% Software Engineering, Data Engineering, and an empathetic understanding of the end-user.
