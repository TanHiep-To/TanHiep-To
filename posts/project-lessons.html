Lessons learned from shipping AI projects

What I learned from building RAG systems, recommender pipelines, and ML products.

May 2026 Tags: Projects · Delivery · MLOps Reading time: 4 min

Transitioning from academic exercises to shipping production AI systems requires a profound shift in mindset. Every project—whether it's building a RAG architecture, deploying a recommender pipeline, or releasing a core ML product—has reinforced the same core philosophy: evaluate early, ship reliably, and always optimize for the end-user's experience over architectural purity.

What Consistently Worked

Across various deployments, these practices have proven to be non-negotiable:

  • Define Success Before Writing Code: Establish rigid success metrics (p99 latency limits, relevance scores, accuracy thresholds) before training any models. If you don't know what "good" looks like, you will never finish tuning.
  • Reproducibility is King: Build reproducible data pipelines from day one. Track experiment lineage meticulously. If you can't exactly recreate the model you shipped last Tuesday, you have a major operational risk.
  • Defensive Evaluation: Use lightweight, automated evaluation suites to catch regressions immediately. Your CI/CD pipeline must include model evaluation, not just unit tests for code.
  • Cross-Functional Alignment: Collaborate with product managers and UX designers early. An accurate model with terrible latency or confusing UI will be perceived as a failure by the user.

The Reality of Collaboration

The most successful projects I’ve been part of shared one common trait: a unified, highly visible dashboard. By putting model metrics (precision, recall), system health (latency, error rates), and business metrics (user click-throughs, feedback scores) in one place, engineering and product teams instantly aligned on priorities. It removes the friction of arguing over whether to focus on model accuracy or system speed.

Evaluation Habits That Save Time

  • The Golden Offline Set: Maintain a strictly versioned offline evaluation dataset with heavily scrutinized baselines. Never alter this set without team consensus.
  • Canary Releases: Never route 100% of traffic to a new ranking model or LLM endpoint. Always use canary releases (e.g., 5% of traffic) to monitor for unexpected edge cases in the wild.
  • Documenting Trade-offs: Machine learning is the art of trade-offs. Whenever making a decision that increases accuracy at the cost of latency or compute budget, document the specific reasoning in an Architecture Decision Record (ADR).

Looking Forward

My ongoing goal is to continue bridging the crucial gap between advanced ML research and robust production systems—turning theoretical academic ideas into practical, scalable AI products that deliver measurable value to users.
