Notes from building an AI search recommender

A realistic snapshot of what worked when shipping recommendation systems.

May 2026 Tags: Recsys · RAG · Ranking Reading time: 4 min

While working as a Junior AI Engineer at First AI, I played a key role in building recommendation and ranking pipelines for our core AI search features. The biggest revelation was that production systems are less about deploying fancy, state-of-the-art models and more about establishing reliable feedback loops, enabling fast iterations, and driving measurable business impact.

What I Learned Quickly in Production

Transitioning from academic datasets to noisy production logs forced a shift in perspective:

  • Metrics Over Models: Before writing any modeling code, establish clear offline evaluation metrics. We focused heavily on precision@k, recall@k, and diversity. If a metric couldn't map to user engagement, we didn't track it.
  • The Personalization Trap: Personalization sounds great on paper, but it only helps if you have clean, abundant user signals. We learned that a strong, generalized baseline often outperforms poorly tuned personalized models.
  • Retrieval > Generation: In AI search, the retrieval step is the bottleneck. The best generation model cannot fix bad context. We shifted resources to improve document parsing and vector search recall before touching the LLM prompt.
  • Offline Eval is a Superpower: Offline evaluation saves weeks of wasted online experimentation. Building a robust offline eval pipeline allowed us to discard bad ideas in minutes instead of waiting for days of A/B test data.

How We Practically Improved Results

Instead of replacing our core architecture, we focused on high-leverage incremental changes. We introduced a lightweight cross-encoder reranker which dramatically improved the top-5 relevance without skyrocketing latency.

We also overhauled our feature logging. By adding a daily automated evaluation report, it became trivial to spot regressions overnight and validate new feature engineering ideas the very next morning.

A Scalable Workflow for AI Teams

  • Hypothesis-Driven Backlog: Every task in our sprint had to be a hypothesis tied to a specific metric improvement.
  • Small Batch Testing: We ran small batch offline experiments to validate the "happy path" before committing any code to the online testing environment.
  • The Rollback Plan: We never launched a ranking change without a one-click rollback plan, ensuring that if engagement tanked, we could recover instantly.

Key Takeaway

The most impactful work I delivered was not a single, complex model, but rather a robust engineering system: clean data pipelines, interpretable metrics, and repeatable, fast experiments.

Adopting this systems-thinking mindset kept the team aligned and helped us deliver steady, compounding improvements without falling into the trap of overfitting to short-term A/B test noise.
