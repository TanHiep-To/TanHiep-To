Learning

A learning routine that sticks

How I balance studying, coding, and research projects.

May 2026 Tags: Learning · Systems Reading time: 4 min

The most common challenge in software engineering is finding the time to stay updated while delivering on sprint commitments. The routine that finally worked for me relies on a structured cycle: learn, apply, and reflect. Instead of passively reading tutorials, I pick one specific, scoped learning goal per week, immediately apply it to a tangible mini-project, and formally document the outcome.

The Weekly Structure: Predictability is Key

I organize my week to ensure learning doesn't get pushed to the weekend:

  • Mon–Tue (Absorb): I dedicate my learning blocks to absorbing new concepts. This involves reading papers, watching deep-dive talks, and outlining notes. The goal isn't to master the topic, but to understand its mental model.
  • Wed–Thu (Apply): This is the crucial step. I force myself to write code using the new concept. It could be a small prototype, a standalone script, or a new branch on an existing project. If I'm learning about vector databases, I build a tiny script that indexes a few documents and runs a similarity search.
  • Fri (Reflect): I review the code, clean it up, and write a short summary documenting what worked, what failed, and the edge cases I discovered. This solidifies the knowledge.

How I Choose Topics Strategically

I avoid "learning for the sake of learning." Instead, I pick topics strictly aligned with my current or upcoming work. When building RAG systems, my weekly topics focused on narrow areas like retrieval evaluation frameworks (RAGAS) or specific reranking strategies. When I transitioned to recommender pipelines, I pivoted my study to ranking loss functions and offline evaluation metrics.

Why This Framework Actually Works

Applying knowledge within 48 hours of acquiring it dramatically improves retention. Furthermore, the Friday reflection forces clarity. If I can't write a coherent summary, it means I don't understand the topic well enough yet.

Over time, these weekly cycles compound. You don't just gain knowledge; you build a searchable, personal portfolio of small experiments. This repository becomes an invaluable resource when tackling complex production issues down the line, and it is incredibly easy to share with peers or showcase in interviews.
