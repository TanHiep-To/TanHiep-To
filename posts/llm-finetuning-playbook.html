LLM fine-tuning playbook for domain adaptation

From data scope to evaluation loops—what to get right on each training run.

Apr 2026 Tags: LLMs · Training Reading time: 4 min

Fine-tuning Large Language Models (LLMs) is rapidly becoming an essential skill for modern AI engineers. However, the boundary between making a model "smarter" in a specific domain and causing "catastrophic forgetting" is incredibly thin. This playbook summarizes my battle-tested experience in fine-tuning models ranging from Llama and Mistral to specialized models tailored for specific languages and domains.

Step 1: Data Quality Dramatically Outweighs Quantity

A common misconception is that you need millions of samples to successfully fine-tune an LLM. In reality, when utilizing parameter-efficient techniques like LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) or QLoRA, you only need 1,000 to 5,000 **exceptionally high-quality** data samples to see a profound improvement. I typically allocate 70% of my project time purely to data cleansing: deduplicating responses, standardizing instruction formats, and rigorously verifying the logical accuracy of every prompt-response pair.

Step 2: Effective Parameter Strategy

Avoid Full Fine-Tuning unless you have access to a massive cluster of high-end GPUs. LoRA is an absolute lifesaver. I usually start with a rank (r) of 8 or 16 and an alpha of 32. By only updating the Query and Value matrices within the Attention mechanism, the model successfully retains its broad foundational knowledge while successfully adapting to the specific style and terminology of your target domain.

Step 3: Evaluation - Do Not Just Look at the Loss Curve

A decreasing training loss does not automatically equate to a better model. I have encountered scenarios where the loss was incredibly low, but the model suffered from severe text repetition. You must build a "vibe check" dataset—a collection of the most challenging edge-case questions in your domain. After every epoch, generate inferences for this set and use a superior model (like GPT-4) as an LLM-as-a-Judge to score the outputs based on accuracy, tone, and strict adherence to formatting constraints.

Step 4: Preventing Generalization Degradation

A practical trick I rely on is mixing approximately 5-10% of general-purpose instruction data (such as the Open-Orca dataset) into my highly specialized training set. This acts as a necessary reminder to the model that it is still a versatile, intelligent assistant, preventing it from devolving into a narrow "parrot" that can only respond to a highly restricted set of prompts.

Fine-tuning is a process of continuous trial and error. Do not be discouraged if your first model checkpoint hallucinates or responds nonsensically. Adjust your Learning Rate, deeply inspect your training data, and try again.
