Learning Journey

My learning journey in AI

From foundational coursework to research-driven GenAI systems.

May 2026 Tags: Learning · AI · MSc Reading time: 4 min

My academic path began firmly rooted in core computer science foundations—mastering algorithms, data structures, and distributed systems. However, the pivotal turning point occurred during my junior year when I participated in undergraduate research on Generative AI. Witnessing a machine learning model autonomously generate creative outputs for the first time made me realize I didn't just want to write code; I wanted to teach machines how to "think." This passion naturally evolved into my pursuit of a Master's degree in Computer Science at VNU-HCM, where I bridge rigorous theory with practical applications.

Phase 1: Overcoming the Foundation Overload

My early days in Machine Learning were genuinely daunting. The complex formulas of linear algebra, calculus, and probability statistics often felt overwhelming. However, I realized that "blind coding" (simply calling library functions without understanding the underlying math) would only lead to a dead end. I dedicated six entire months to writing foundational algorithms from scratch using NumPy instead of relying on scikit-learn. This perseverance established an invaluable launchpad for tackling more complex architectures later on.

Phase 2: Choosing a Specialized Path

Once my foundational knowledge was solid, I delved deeply into Deep Learning, Computer Vision, and Natural Language Processing (NLP). Joining the university's research lab allowed me to work hands-on with diffusion models like Stable Diffusion. The challenge of fine-tuning models to ensure the generated results were both highly accurate and computationally optimized taught me a profound lesson about balancing model quality with resource constraints. Graduating with a perfect 10/10 score on my undergraduate thesis was the sweet reward for this intense period of focus.

Phase 3: From Lab to Production

The most significant difference between an academic lab and an enterprise environment is the absolute necessity for stability. When applying research concepts like Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) or parameter-efficient LLM fine-tuning (LoRA, QLoRA) in the real world, I constantly encountered issues with high latency, data drift, and edge cases. At First AI, while building a Recommender System and later applying ML to cybersecurity, I learned how to design robust offline evaluation systems, write extensive monitoring scripts, and build a flawless Data Pipeline long before even thinking about training a model.

The Most Valuable Lessons Learned

  • There is no clean data in the real world: Spend 80% of your time on Data Engineering and truly understanding your data before training any model.
  • Consistency trumps intensity: Don't try to pull all-nighters to master a framework. Study 1-2 hours every day, but do it consistently and immediately apply it to a mini-project.
  • Share your knowledge: Actively share what you are building, seek feedback from senior engineers, and participate in community discussions. Explaining concepts to others is the fastest way to solidify your own understanding.

This journey has never been a straight line. There are times when debugging a model leads to pure frustration, but it is precisely in those moments that I find entirely new approaches. Be patient, and take it one step at a time.
