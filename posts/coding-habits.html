Keeping a sustainable coding habit

A practical routine that keeps quality high without burnout.

May 2026 Tags: Habits · Productivity Reading time: 4 min

In the software engineering world, we often hear the phrase "Consistency is key." But how do you actually maintain that consistency when your daily workload is already overwhelming? After years of oscillating between all-night coding marathons and extended periods of burnout, I have developed a system of habits that allows me to write code consistently every day while maintaining a high level of enthusiasm.

1. The 90-Minute Deep Work Rule

I realized early on that the human brain is not designed to focus continuously for 8 hours. Instead of forcing myself to sit at a desk all day, I divide my time into 90-minute Deep Work blocks. During these 90 minutes, my phone is on airplane mode, and all Slack or email notifications are muted. The difference in output between 90 minutes of absolute focus and 4 hours of distracted work is staggering. You will solve your most complex logical problems during this dedicated window.

2. Leave Breadcrumbs for Tomorrow

This is a small habit that completely transformed my productivity: Before ending a coding session, never stop at a point where a task is 100% complete. I intentionally leave a small task unfinished or write a clear comment detailing exactly what the next step is. The next morning, instead of wasting 30 minutes trying to remember where I left off or deciding what to do next, I only need 1 minute to dive straight into that unfinished task. This completely eliminates the friction of starting.

3. Review Your Own Code After 24 Hours

The code you write at 11:00 PM is rarely as brilliant as you think it is. My habit is to spend 15-20 minutes every morning reviewing the commits I pushed the night before. This is when I discover clearer variable names, more optimal logic paths, or redundant code blocks. Self-critique elevates your engineering skills faster than any online course ever could.

4. Balance "Doing" and "Learning"

If you only do what you already know, your skills will stagnate. I strictly maintain a 70/20/10 rule: 70% of my time is spent coding familiar tasks, 20% is dedicated to applying new technologies to existing tasks, and 10% is reserved entirely for pure research (such as reading AI papers or experimenting with a new programming language).

Finally, never forget that software engineering is a marathon, not a sprint. Maintain a healthy body and a clear mind so you can sustain a long and fulfilling career in this industry.
